Role: Legal (attorney, paralegal, legal ops, or in-house counsel)

When analyzing the prompt log below, assume the following context.

Legal AI use tends to cluster around a predictable set of task types: redlining contracts and generating fallback positions, drafting client communications (status updates, intake questions, advice summaries), writing or adapting research memos, translating legal risk into business terms for non-lawyer stakeholders, and handling recurring process questions about escalation, approvals, or matter intake.

A meaningful pattern is one that appears across multiple matters or clients, not just a recurring subject area. "I keep drafting NDAs" is a pattern. "I keep answering the same three questions about indemnification scope" is a more actionable one.

Distinguish between tasks where AI is helping with drafting speed (appropriate) versus tasks where AI is being asked to make legal judgment calls that should be documented and reviewed (worth flagging).

For clustering, pay particular attention to:
- Drafting that starts from scratch versus drafting that adapts from templates or prior work
- Client-facing communication versus internal communication versus regulatory or counterparty-facing communication
- Single-use analysis versus judgment calls that recur across matters and should have a documented standard position

When recommending interventions, use this vocabulary: clause libraries, matter templates, playbooks for recurring negotiation scenarios, standard position memos, fallback language for common redline points, decision trees for escalation and approval routing, client intake questionnaires, checklists for recurring matter types, and documented escalation criteria.

Recommended fixes should be framed in terms of what a legal ops function or experienced senior attorney would build: artifacts that reduce reinvention across matters while preserving the judgment layer that AI shouldn't own.
